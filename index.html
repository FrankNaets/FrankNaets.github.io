About

I'm a post-doctoral researcher in the MOD group of the mechanical engineering department at the KU Leuven. My research focusses on efficient modeling for (nonlinear) mechanical applications. These models are being applied both in forward simulation and inverse problems (like design optimization and state-estimation).

I completed my bachelor and master in Mechanical Engineering at the KU Leuven. I received my PhD on model reduction for flexible multibody simulation from the KU Leuven as well. During my PhD I spent six months at the LIM group from the University of A Coruna in Spain working on state/input/parameter estimation for mechanical systems. I also spent eight months at the Farhat Research Group at Stanford University during my post-doctoral scholarship, working on local model order reduction for (nonlinear) mechanical models.

In the near future I will host several of the research codes I am using on my Github. These codes should be sufficient to recreate the results from my (new) papers. This code can be reused and altered by anyone interested to do so (in exchange for a mere acknowledgement).

Multibody simulation

Multibody simulation provides tools to analyse the interaction between different structural components in a machine. My research focuses on how these models can be described efficiently while taking component deformation into account. I am applying these flexible multibody models in state-estimation applications to obtain a detailed view on full machine behavior from a limited set of measurements.

Model Reduction

Model order reduction provides generic techniques to transform expensive high-fidelity models into lower-cost models for specific purposes. My research focuses specifically on the development and exploitation of reduced order models for flexible multibody and nonlinear finite element models for structural simulations. My primary aim is to obtain methods which can be used without training through initial full order simulations.

Virtual Sensing

State-estimation techniques allow to combine the data obtained from simulation models and measurements in order to infer information which cannot be measured directly. In this work I employ first principle models (analytical, multibody or (reduced) finite element) in a state-estimation setting to estimate parameters and inputs on mechanical systems. The applications range from automotive to highly-dynamic industrial machinery.
